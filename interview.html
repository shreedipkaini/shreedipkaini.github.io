How to Use This Document

This guide follows the structure of Moderne Physik 2 by Sara Konrad and Matthias Bartelmann (Institute for Theoretical Physics, Heidelberg University). Prof. Bartelmann is in the cosmology group at Heidelberg — the same group as Prof. Heisenberg — so this textbook is essentially the curated Heidelberg view of modern physics. The TOC of this book maps almost exactly onto the territory Prof. Eichhorn (quantum gravity / QFT) and Prof. Heisenberg (cosmology / modified gravity) will examine.

Question depth, calibrated to the actual interview style

From the interview reference document we have (a successful Heidelberg MSc interview transcript), the depth required is:

  • Keywords beat elaborate explanations. Quick conceptual response, not full derivation.
  • Headline answer first, follow-ups invited.
  • Symmetry arguments win points. “Discretized because of boundary conditions / symmetry.”
  • Pointing in the right direction is enough. (“We make a typical ansatz” — they’ll ask what kind only if they want detail.)
  • Be honest when you set ℏ = 1 or use natural units. Examiners notice and reward the awareness.
  • Breadth > depth. A wide span of topics will be probed; each one only ~1–2 minutes.

Chapter 1 — Introduction (Einleitende Bemerkungen)

What this chapter covers

  • Why we study modern physics (1920s+ developments)
  • Definition: physics is “modern” if it includes relativity and quantum theory
  • Role of theoretical physics: find mathematical structures for experimental phenomena
  • Foundational ideas: space, time, dynamics, forces

Subject knowledge expected

  • Theoretical physics maps phenomena onto mathematical structures
  • Modern physics = relativity + quantum theory
  • Field theories assign their own dynamics to forces, unlike Newtonian mechanics

Likely questions

⭐ Q1.1: Why is modern physics called modern?

Model answer: Because it incorporates relativity and quantum theory — both developed after 1900. Classical (pre-modern) physics treats space, time, and matter as absolute and continuous; modern physics revises all three.

⭐ Q1.2: What is the role of theoretical physics?

Model answer: To find mathematical structures that map experimental phenomena, and to derive testable predictions from them. Mathematics is the language; experiment is the arbiter.

⭐ Q1.3: Why is GPS impossible without general relativity?

Model answer: GPS satellites orbit at high altitude (weak gravity, time runs faster) and high speed (special-relativistic time dilation, time runs slower). The two effects don’t cancel — net is about  + 38 μs/day that must be corrected, or positions would drift by  ∼ 10 km/day.

Chapter 2 — Classical Mechanics 1 (Newton’sche Axiome)

What this chapter covers

  • Aims of classical mechanics: predict motion given forces
  • Concepts of space and time (absolute in Newton, relative in Einstein)
  • Newton’s three axioms
  • Inertial frames and Galilean invariance

Subject knowledge expected

  • All three of Newton’s axioms, including the fundamental form F = dp/dt
  • Why we prefer momentum over acceleration
  • Definition of an inertial frame
  • Galilean transformations and which quantities are invariant

Likely questions

⭐⭐⭐ Q2.1: State Newton’s second law.

Model answer: In its most general form, F = dp/dt. The familiar F = ma is a special case when mass is constant. The momentum form survives in rocket dynamics (variable mass) and in special relativity (p = γmv).

Follow-up Q2.1a: Where does F = ma fail?

Rockets — the dm/dt term gives the thrust force urel |dm/dt| that produces Δv = urelln (m0/mf), Tsiolkovsky’s equation. Also relativistic mechanics: F and a are not parallel in general.

Follow-up Q2.1b: When does Newton’s third law fail?

Magnetic forces between two moving charges are not generally equal and opposite at the same instant. The “missing” momentum is carried by the electromagnetic field — total matter + field momentum is still conserved.

⭐⭐ Q2.2: Define an inertial frame.

Model answer: A reference frame in which a free particle (no forces acting on it) moves in a straight line at constant velocity — i.e., where Newton’s first law holds. Inertial frames are related to each other by Galilean transformations (in Newtonian mechanics) or Lorentz transformations (in special relativity).

Follow-up Q2.2a: Are Newton’s laws Lorentz-invariant?

No. Newton’s laws are Galilean-invariant. Lorentz invariance requires reformulation: F = dp/dt with relativistic p = γmv, plus the full four-vector formalism. This was Einstein’s motivation in 1905.

⭐⭐ Q2.3: What are pseudo-forces? Are they “real”?

Model answer: Pseudo-forces (centrifugal, Coriolis, Euler) appear in non-inertial frames and vanish in inertial frames. They are mathematically real (they affect equations of motion in that frame) but disappear under a change of frame. Their deepest extension: Einstein’s equivalence principle — even gravity is locally a pseudo-force, eliminable in a free-fall frame. This is the foundation of general relativity.

Examiner note (Heisenberg): ⭐⭐⭐ This equivalence-principle connection is gold for Heisenberg. Always close the pseudo-force question with “…which is why Einstein concluded gravity is geometry, not a force.”

Chapter 3 — Groups (Einschub: Gruppen)

What this chapter covers

  • Definition of a group
  • Representations of groups
  • The Galilei group as a concrete example

Subject knowledge expected

  • The four group axioms (closure, associativity, identity, inverse)
  • Difference between discrete and continuous (Lie) groups
  • What a representation is — a way of “realizing” the group as matrices acting on a space
  • Galilean group: rotations, boosts, translations in space and time

Likely questions

⭐⭐ Q3.1: What is a group? Why do we care in physics?

Model answer: A group G is a set with an operation ⋅ satisfying: (i) closure (g1 ⋅ g2 ∈ G), (ii) associativity, (iii) identity element exists, (iv) inverses exist. We care because groups encode symmetries, and symmetries (by Noether’s theorem) give conservation laws and structural constraints on physical theories.

Follow-up Q3.1a: What’s a Lie group?

A group whose elements form a continuous, differentiable manifold — you can parametrize transformations continuously (e.g., rotation angle). All transformations arise as G = exp (T) from generators T in the corresponding Lie algebra.

Follow-up Q3.1b: Give physical examples of important groups.

SO(3) rotations of 3D space; SO(3,1) Lorentz group; SU(2) (covers spin-$\tfrac12$ rotations and the double cover of SO(3)); U(1) for electromagnetism; the Galilean group; the Poincaré group; SU(3) × SU(2) × U(1) of the Standard Model.

⭐⭐⭐ Q3.2: Why are rotations described by a group?

Model answer: Composition of two rotations is another rotation (closure); the identity is “no rotation”; every rotation has an inverse; composition is associative. So rotations satisfy all four group axioms. The rotation group is SO(3), three-dimensional, parametrized by three Euler angles (or axis-angle).

Follow-up Q3.2a: Compare SO(3) and SU(2).

Same Lie algebra ([Ji,Jj] = iℏϵijkJk), but SU(2) is the double cover of SO(3). Two elements of SU(2) (e.g., U and  − U) correspond to the same rotation. Spinors live in SU(2) and pick up a minus sign under 2π rotation; you need 4π to return to identity. This is measurable (neutron interferometry).

⭐⭐ Q3.3: What is a representation of a group?

Model answer: A homomorphism R : G → GL(V) — i.e., a mapping of group elements to invertible linear operators on a vector space, preserving multiplication: R(g1g2) = R(g1)R(g2). Different representations describe different physical quantities: scalars (trivial rep), vectors (fundamental rep), tensors, spinors. Spin 0, 1/2, 1, etc. of elementary particles correspond to different representations of the Lorentz group.

Examiner note (Eichhorn): ⭐⭐⭐ Eichhorn’s Chapter 2 of her QFT notes is literally devoted to this. She’ll likely ask about representations directly or via the question “Why do we label particles by mass and spin?”

Chapter 4 — Classical Mechanics 2: Analytical Mechanics

What this chapter covers

  • Noether’s theorems
  • Constraints (holonomic vs. non-holonomic)
  • Extremal principles: action, Euler-Lagrange equation

Subject knowledge expected

  • Statement and derivation of Noether’s theorem
  • The three classic applications: time translation → energy, space translation → momentum, rotation → angular momentum
  • Holonomic vs. non-holonomic constraints; examples of each
  • Principle of stationary action; Euler-Lagrange derivation
  • Lagrangian for a free particle, a particle in a potential, a pendulum

Likely questions

⭐⭐⭐ Q4.1: State the principle of stationary action.

Model answer: The action S = ∫L dt where L = T − V is stationary (δS = 0) along physical trajectories with fixed endpoints. This single principle gives equations of motion that generalize to field theory, relativity, and quantum mechanics. F = ma doesn’t generalize; δS = 0 does.

Follow-up Q4.1a: Derive the Euler-Lagrange equation.

Vary q → q + δq with δq vanishing at endpoints. $\delta S = \int \left[\dfrac{\partial L}{\partial q}\delta q + \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q}\delta\dot q\right] dt$. Integrate the second term by parts; boundary terms vanish. $\delta S = \int \left[\dfrac{\partial L}{\partial q} - \dfrac{d}{dt}\dfrac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q}\right]\delta q\, dt = 0$ for arbitrary δq. By the fundamental lemma of calculus of variations: $\boxed{\dfrac{d}{dt}\dfrac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q} - \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial q} = 0}$

Follow-up Q4.1b: Why is δS = 0 “natural”?

Because in Feynman’s path-integral formulation of QM, the amplitude is ∫𝒟q eiS/ℏ. All paths contribute, but only those near δS = 0 interfere constructively in the classical limit ℏ → 0. The classical action principle emerges from QM.

⭐⭐⭐ Q4.2: State Noether’s theorem.

Model answer: Every continuous symmetry of the action implies a conserved quantity. If S is invariant under q → q + ϵ δq, then $Q = \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial\dot q}\,\delta q$ is conserved: dQ/dt = 0.

Follow-up Q4.2a: How does time-translation symmetry give energy conservation?

If L doesn’t depend explicitly on t, the corresponding conserved quantity is the Hamiltonian H = pq̇ − L. For standard kinetic energies (quadratic in q̇) and velocity-independent potentials, H = T + V = E. So time-translation invariance ⇔ energy conservation.

Follow-up Q4.2b: Where does energy conservation fail?

In cosmology. The expanding universe breaks time-translation symmetry — the universe at time t differs from time t′. By Noether: no symmetry, no conservation. Photons redshift, losing energy with no compensating gain. This is not a flaw but a consequence of Noether’s theorem working correctly.

Follow-up Q4.2c: What conserved quantity comes from spatial translation? Rotation?

Spatial translation → momentum p. Rotation about an axis → component of angular momentum along that axis.

Examiner note (Eichhorn): ⭐⭐⭐ Eichhorn led an Emmy Noether Group. Noether’s theorem is the most likely topic. Know all three applications cold and be able to derive the theorem itself.

⭐⭐ Q4.3: Distinguish holonomic and non-holonomic constraints.

Model answer: Holonomic constraints can be written as f(q,t) = 0 — they reduce the number of independent coordinates. Bead on a wire: the wire is the constraint, one coordinate suffices. Non-holonomic constraints involve velocities and cannot be integrated to a coordinate relation. Rolling ball on a plane: the rolling condition relates linear and angular velocity, but the final orientation depends on the path, not just endpoints.

Chapter 5 — Special Relativity (Spezielle Relativitätstheorie)

What this chapter covers

  • Speed of light as universal constant
  • From Galilei to Poincaré symmetry
  • The light cone and Minkowski causal structure
  • Consequences: time dilation, length contraction, Lorentz transformations

Subject knowledge expected

  • The two postulates of SR
  • Lorentz transformations
  • Time dilation, length contraction, relativity of simultaneity
  • Four-vectors and the spacetime interval ds2
  • Energy-momentum relation E2 = (pc)2 + (mc2)2
  • Minkowski metric ημν

Likely questions

⭐⭐⭐ Q5.1: State the two postulates of special relativity.

Model answer:

  1. Principle of relativity: Laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames.
  2. Constancy of the speed of light: c is the same in every inertial frame, independent of the motion of source or observer.

Follow-up Q5.1a: Why is the second postulate revolutionary?

Because it contradicts Galilean velocity addition. In Galilean mechanics, if you chase a light beam, you’d see it move slower. Experimentally (Michelson-Morley 1887) the speed of light is the same. This forces a revision of how we relate frames — Lorentz transformations instead of Galilean.

⭐⭐⭐ Q5.2: Derive time dilation.

Model answer: Light clock argument. Two mirrors separated by L; a photon bounces. In the rest frame, period T0 = 2L/c. In a lab frame where the clock moves with velocity v perpendicular to the mirror axis, the photon travels a diagonal of length $\sqrt{L^2 + (vT/2)^2}$ per half-period. Total path  = cT, so $2\sqrt{L^2 + (vT/2)^2} = cT$, giving $T = T_0/\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2} = \gamma T_0$. A moving clock runs slow by factor γ.

Follow-up Q5.2a: What’s length contraction?

A moving rod of rest length L0 has lab-frame length L = L0/γ along its direction of motion. Transverse dimensions unchanged. Derivable from time dilation plus the constancy of c.

Follow-up Q5.2b: How do velocities add relativistically?

$u' = \dfrac{u - v}{1 - uv/c^2}$. If u = c, then u′ = c — speed of light is conserved under the transformation, as required by the second postulate.

⭐⭐⭐ Q5.3: Write Lorentz transformations for a boost along x.

Model answer: x′ = γ(x−vt),  t′ = γ(t−vx/c2),  y′ = y,  z′ = z with $\gamma = 1/\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}$. These preserve the spacetime interval ds2 = c2dt2 − dx2 − dy2 − dz2, which is the analog of Euclidean distance in Minkowski space.

Follow-up Q5.3a: Define a four-vector.

An object xμ = (ct,x,y,z) that transforms under Lorentz transformations as x′μ = Λ νμxν and whose inner product with itself xμxμ = ημνxμxν is Lorentz-invariant. Other examples: four-momentum pμ = (E/c,p), four-velocity, four-current.

Follow-up Q5.3b: What is pμpμ?

pμpμ = E2/c2 − p2 = m2c2. This gives the famous relation E2 = (pc)2 + (mc2)2. For massless particles (m = 0): E = pc.

⭐⭐ Q5.4: What is the Lorentz group? Why SO(3,1)?

Model answer: The group of transformations preserving the Minkowski metric ημν = diag(+1,−1,−1,−1). It’s SO(3,1) because the metric has signature (3,1) — three spatial dimensions (minus signs) and one time dimension (plus sign). Not SO(4) (Euclidean 4D) — Minkowski geometry is different. Generators: 3 rotations + 3 boosts = 6 generators total.

Examiner note (Heisenberg): ⭐⭐⭐ Heisenberg works in GR — covariant formalism is her language. Be fluent with index notation, metric, four-vectors.

Examiner note (Eichhorn): ⭐⭐⭐ Eichhorn’s QFT notes Chapter 2 derives the Lorentz group structure, then uses it to classify all possible particle representations (scalar, spinor, vector). This is exactly her favorite topic.

Chapter 6 — Electrodynamics (Elektrodynamik)

What this chapter covers

  • The electromagnetic field tensor Fμν
  • Action of electrodynamics ($\mathcal{L} = -\tfrac14 F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu}$)
  • Maxwell’s equations from variational principle
  • Consequences: wave solutions, gauge invariance

Subject knowledge expected

  • Maxwell’s equations in vector form and covariant form
  • Why the displacement current is needed
  • Lagrangian density of EM
  • How E and B are encoded in Fμν
  • Gauge invariance Aμ → Aμ + ∂μχ

Likely questions

⭐⭐⭐ Q6.1: Write Maxwell’s equations.

Model answer (SI): ∇ ⋅ E = ρ/ϵ0,  ∇ ⋅ B = 0,  ∇ × E =  − ∂tB,  ∇ × B = μ0J + μ0ϵ0 ∂tE Gauss (charges source E), no monopoles, Faraday (changing B induces E), Ampère-Maxwell (currents and changing E induce B).

Follow-up Q6.1a: Why is the displacement current needed?

Without μ0ϵ0 ∂tE, ∇ ⋅ (∇×B) = μ0∇ ⋅ J, which must equal zero — but continuity says ∇ ⋅ J =  − ∂ρ/∂t ≠ 0 in general. Adding μ0ϵ0 ∂tE makes it consistent and predicts EM waves traveling at $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0\epsilon_0}$ — Maxwell’s spectacular prediction.

⭐⭐⭐ Q6.2: Write Maxwell’s equations in covariant form.

Model answer: ∂μFμν = μ0Jν  (inhomogeneous: Gauss + Ampère-Maxwell) ∂μF̃μν = 0  (homogeneous: no monopole + Faraday) where Fμν is the antisymmetric field tensor with F0i =  − Ei/c, Fij =  − ϵijkBk, and Jμ = (cρ,J) is the four-current. The four-potential is Aμ = (φ/c,A), and Fμν = ∂μAν − ∂νAμ.

⭐⭐⭐ Q6.3: Write the Lagrangian density of electrodynamics.

Model answer: For free EM field: $\mathcal{L} = -\tfrac14 F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu}$. With matter coupling: $\mathcal{L} = -\tfrac14 F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu} - A_\mu J^\mu$. The Euler-Lagrange equation ∂μ(∂ℒ/∂(∂μAν)) − ∂ℒ/∂Aν = 0 gives back Maxwell’s equations ∂μFμν = Jν. The homogeneous equations follow automatically from F = dA (Bianchi identity).

Follow-up Q6.3a: Why this combination FμνFμν?

Three reasons: (1) Lorentz invariant (tensor contracted with itself). (2) Gauge invariant (F is unchanged under Aμ → Aμ + ∂μχ). (3) Lowest-order kinetic term with two derivatives, avoiding Ostrogradsky instabilities from higher derivatives.

⭐⭐⭐ Q6.4: What is gauge invariance?

Model answer: The transformation Aμ → Aμ + ∂μχ leaves all observable quantities (E, B, and Fμν) unchanged, so it’s an exact symmetry of the theory. The vector potential Aμ is “redundant”; physical quantities are gauge-invariant.

Follow-up Q6.4a: Why does gauge invariance require charge conservation?

The interaction term AμJμ is not gauge invariant by itself — under Aμ → Aμ + ∂μχ, it picks up ∂μχJμ. Integration by parts gives  − χ ∂μJμ. For the action to be gauge-invariant for arbitrary χ, we need ∂μJμ = 0 — charge conservation. So gauge symmetry ⇒ charge conservation. (This is Noether’s theorem for local symmetries.)

Examiner note: ⭐⭐⭐ Both examiners care deeply. Eichhorn’s QFT course derives U(1) gauge symmetry as the origin of electromagnetism. Heisenberg notes the analog “diffeomorphism invariance” in GR plays the same role.

Chapter 7 — Quantum Mechanics 1 (Foundations)

What this chapter covers

  • Reasons for a new mechanics (photoelectric effect, blackbody, atomic spectra)
  • Wave-particle duality
  • Quantum mechanical states

Subject knowledge expected

  • Failures of classical physics that motivated QM
  • de Broglie hypothesis: λ = h/p
  • Wave functions and probability interpretation |ψ|2
  • Superposition principle

Likely questions

⭐⭐ Q7.1: Why do we need quantum mechanics?

Model answer: Classical physics fails for atoms and radiation:

  • Blackbody radiation: classical equipartition gives the Rayleigh-Jeans law and the UV catastrophe. Planck resolved it by quantizing energy E = hν.
  • Photoelectric effect: light’s particle nature (Einstein 1905) — photons of energy hν.
  • Atomic stability: classical EM predicts an electron orbiting a nucleus radiates energy and spirals in within  ∼ 10−11 s. Real atoms are stable — Bohr (1913) postulated quantized orbits.
  • Atomic spectra: discrete lines, not a continuum — explained by quantized energy levels.

Follow-up Q7.1a: What is the de Broglie hypothesis?

Every particle of momentum p has an associated wavelength λ = h/p. Confirmed for electrons by the Davisson-Germer experiment (1927) — electron diffraction off a nickel crystal.

⭐⭐ Q7.2: What is the meaning of the wavefunction?

Model answer: |ψ(x,t)|2 is the probability density of finding the particle at position x at time t (Born rule). The wavefunction itself is complex; only |ψ|2 is observable. Normalization: ∫|ψ|2 dx = 1.

Follow-up Q7.2a: What’s the probability current?

$\mathbf{J} = \dfrac{\hbar}{2mi}(\psi^*\nabla\psi - \psi\nabla\psi^*) = \dfrac{\hbar}{m}\mathrm{Im}(\psi^*\nabla\psi)$. Combined with ρ = |ψ|2, satisfies a continuity equation ∂ρ/∂t + ∇ ⋅ J = 0 — probability is locally conserved.

⭐ Q7.3: How does the Bohr model fall short of quantum mechanics?

Model answer: Bohr’s model postulates quantized angular momentum (L = nℏ) ad hoc. It gives correct H spectrum but:

  • Cannot explain why other quantum numbers exist (l, m, s).
  • Cannot handle multi-electron atoms.
  • Doesn’t predict spectral line intensities or selection rules.
  • Has no consistent treatment of spin.

QM derives quantization from solving the Schrödinger equation with appropriate boundary conditions. The energy quantization emerges, not assumed.

Follow-up Q7.3a: Why don’t electrons fall into the nucleus?

In QM, the wavefunction must satisfy ψ(r→0) regularity. For the hydrogen atom, the ground-state wavefunction is ψ100 ∝ e−r/a0 — finite at r = 0, with most probability at r ∼ a0 (Bohr radius). Classically the electron would radiate and spiral in; quantum mechanically there is no orbit, just a probability cloud with a minimum-energy state.

Examiner note: The interview reference document showed this question explicitly: “Why do we not fall through the ground?” Answer: boundary conditions on the radial equation + Pauli exclusion.

Chapter 8 — Operators in Quantum Mechanics

What this chapter covers

  • Linear operators on Hilbert space
  • Bases and representations
  • Quantum-mechanical operators (position, momentum, Hamiltonian)

Subject knowledge expected

  • What makes an operator linear
  • Eigenvalues and eigenvectors
  • Hermitian (self-adjoint) operators have real eigenvalues, orthogonal eigenvectors
  • Position x̂ and momentum p̂ =  − iℏ ∂x in position representation
  • Canonical commutator [x̂,p̂] = iℏ

Likely questions

⭐⭐⭐ Q8.1: Why must physical observables be represented by Hermitian operators?

Model answer: Two reasons:

  1. Real eigenvalues: measurements yield real numbers, so eigenvalues of the operator must be real. Hermitian operators are guaranteed to have real eigenvalues.
  2. Orthogonal eigenvectors: different measurement outcomes must correspond to distinguishable (orthogonal) states. Eigenvectors of Hermitian operators with distinct eigenvalues are automatically orthogonal.

The spectral theorem says every Hermitian operator can be diagonalized in an orthonormal basis.

⭐⭐⭐ Q8.2: Compute [x̂,p̂].

Model answer: In position representation p̂ =  − iℏ d/dx. Act on a test function ψ(x): [x̂,p̂]ψ = x̂p̂ψ − p̂x̂ψ =  − iℏxψ′ − (−iℏ)(xψ)′ =  − iℏxψ′ + iℏψ + iℏxψ′ = iℏ ψ. Therefore $\boxed{[\hat x, \hat p] = i\hbar}$.

Follow-up Q8.2a: Compute [L̂x,L̂y].

Using L̂i = ϵijkx̂jp̂k and applying [x̂j,p̂k] = iℏδjk gives [L̂x,L̂y] = iℏL̂z. The angular momentum operators form the SO(3) Lie algebra times iℏ — exact same structure as classical Poisson bracket {Lx, Ly} = Lz.

Follow-up Q8.2b: Why does this force infinite-dimensional Hilbert spaces?

Trace of a commutator is zero: Tr([A,B]) = 0 for finite matrices. But Tr(iℏ 1) = iℏ n ≠ 0 for any finite n. Contradiction. So x̂ and p̂ cannot be represented by finite-dimensional matrices — the Hilbert space is infinite-dimensional. (Stone-von Neumann theorem.)

⭐⭐ Q8.3: What is a basis in QM? Why useful?

Model answer: An orthonormal basis {|n⟩} satisfies ⟨m|n⟩ = δmn and ∑n|n⟩⟨n| = 1 (completeness). Any state can be expanded: |ψ⟩=∑ncn|n⟩ with cn = ⟨n|ψ⟩. Choosing a basis of energy eigenstates makes time evolution trivial: |ψ(t)⟩=∑ncn e−iEnt/ℏ|n⟩. Choosing position basis gives ψ(x) = ⟨x|ψ⟩ — the standard wavefunction.

Likely questions

⭐⭐⭐ Q9.1: State the postulates of quantum mechanics.

Model answer:

  1. State: A physical state is a unit vector |ψ⟩ in a complex Hilbert space.
  2. Observables: Each observable is a Hermitian (self-adjoint) operator Â.
  3. Measurement: Possible outcomes are eigenvalues an of Â. Probability of an is |⟨n|ψ⟩|2 (Born rule). After measurement, the state collapses to |n⟩.
  4. Time evolution: iℏ ∂t|ψ⟩=Ĥ|ψ⟩ (Schrödinger).

Follow-up Q9.1a: Why must time evolution be unitary?

Because Ĥ is Hermitian, Û(t) = e−iĤt/ℏ is unitary, so Û†Û = 1. Unitary evolution preserves inner products (⟨ψ|ϕ⟩ stays constant), so probability is conserved. Classical analog: Liouville’s theorem (phase-space volume preservation).

⭐⭐⭐ Q9.2: How would you solve the hydrogen atom in QM?

Model answer (this exact Q appears in the interview reference doc — keep the same style):

Take the 3D time-independent Schrödinger equation with the Coulomb potential V(r) =  − e2/(4πϵ0r).

Use separation of variables: ψ(r,θ,ϕ) = R(r) Y(θ,ϕ). This separates into a radial equation and an angular equation.

Follow-up Q9.2a: What’s the next step?

For the radial equation, we make a typical ansatz (e.g., R(r) = u(r)/r with u = e−r/a rl v(r), v(r) a polynomial — leads to Laguerre polynomials). For the angular equation, the solutions are spherical harmonics Ylm(θ,ϕ).

Follow-up Q9.2b: What are the boundary conditions?

For the radial equation: ψ must be regular at r = 0 (otherwise it blows up). And ψ → 0 as r → ∞ (otherwise unbounded probability — unphysical, also wouldn’t be normalizable).

Follow-up Q9.2c: Why are the angular momentum values discretized?

Because of symmetry / boundary conditions. The angular coordinates have the ranges θ ∈ [0,π] and ϕ ∈ [0,2π], and continuity of the wavefunction under ϕ → ϕ + 2π forces m to be an integer. The constraint that Ylm be regular at the poles further forces l = 0, 1, 2, … with |m| ≤ l.

Follow-up Q9.2d: How is l discretized?

Eigenvalues of L̂2 are ℏ2l(l+1). (You can quickly drop the ℏ if they ask “natural units?” — answer cheerfully “Yes, ℏ = 1.”)

⭐⭐⭐ Q9.3: Solve the 1D harmonic oscillator.

Model answer: Hamiltonian $\hat H = \dfrac{\hat p^2}{2m} + \tfrac12 m\omega^2 \hat x^2$. Introduce ladder operators $$\hat a = \sqrt{\dfrac{m\omega}{2\hbar}}\left(\hat x + \dfrac{i\hat p}{m\omega}\right), \qquad \hat a^\dagger = \sqrt{\dfrac{m\omega}{2\hbar}}\left(\hat x - \dfrac{i\hat p}{m\omega}\right).$$ Then [â,â†] = 1 and $\hat H = \hbar\omega(\hat a^\dagger \hat a + \tfrac12)$. Energies: $E_n = \hbar\omega(n + \tfrac12)$ for n = 0, 1, 2, …. Ground state has â|0⟩ = 0, giving a Gaussian ψ0(x) ∝ e−mωx2/(2ℏ). The zero-point energy ℏω/2 is non-zero, in contrast to classical mechanics.

Follow-up Q9.3a: Why does the harmonic oscillator matter so much in physics?

Any small oscillation around a stable equilibrium reduces to it (Taylor expansion of V around its minimum). It’s also the building block of QFT: each mode of a quantum field is a harmonic oscillator, and creation/annihilation operators promote excitations into “particles” of the field.

⭐⭐ Q9.4: Expected value of x for an electron in vacuum.

Model answer: (from interview reference) ⟨x⟩ = 0 by symmetry. The free-particle ground state has reflection symmetry, so the expectation value of any odd function of x vanishes.

⭐⭐ Q9.5: What happens to an electron excited in an atom?

Model answer: It decays to a lower energy state by emitting a photon. Rate governed by Fermi’s golden rule: $$\Gamma_{i \to f} = \dfrac{2\pi}{\hbar}\,|\langle f | \hat V | i \rangle|^2\,\rho(E_f)$$ where V̂ is the perturbation and ρ(Ef) the density of final states. The photon energy equals the energy difference Ei − Ef.

⭐ Q9.6: Schrödinger’s cat — what’s the point?

Model answer: A thought experiment illustrating measurement problem: a macroscopic superposition (cat alive + cat dead) is allowed by QM rules but never observed. Resolution mechanisms: decoherence (interaction with environment rapidly destroys superpositions of macroscopic states), interpretations (Copenhagen, many-worlds, hidden variables, etc.).

Chapter 10 — Thermodynamics 1

What this chapter covers

  • Microstates and macrostates
  • Equilibrium
  • State variables vs. process variables
  • Entropy (Boltzmann definition)

Subject knowledge expected

  • Definition of micro/macrostate
  • Equilibrium = maximum entropy
  • State vs. process variables (e.g., T, P, V, S vs. Q, W)
  • S = kBln Ω
  • Connection to information theory

Likely questions

⭐⭐ Q10.1: How do you define temperature for any system?

Model answer: (from interview reference) Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution for a classical gas in equilibrium — the parameter T in P(E) ∝ e−E/kBT is the temperature. More generally and abstractly: $$\dfrac{1}{T} = \dfrac{\partial S}{\partial U}\bigg|_{V,N}$$ i.e., temperature is the rate of change of entropy with respect to internal energy at fixed volume and particle number. Even more loosely: “the mean energy per degree of freedom” $\sim \tfrac12 k_B T$ (equipartition).

Follow-up Q10.1a: How would you define temperature in general?

(from the actual interview!) “It’s the vibrational energy of the molecules or particles.” This is a colloquial but acceptable answer — the precise statement is equipartition: every quadratic degree of freedom carries $\tfrac12 k_B T$.

⭐⭐ Q10.2: What is a microstate vs. a macrostate?

Model answer: A microstate specifies the complete configuration of all degrees of freedom (positions, momenta of all particles, or quantum numbers). A macrostate is specified by macroscopic variables (energy, volume, particle number). Many microstates typically realize the same macrostate; the number of such microstates is Ω, and S = kBln Ω.

⭐⭐ Q10.3: Define entropy.

Model answer: Two equivalent definitions:

  • Boltzmann (microscopic): S = kBln Ω, where Ω is the number of microstates compatible with the given macrostate.
  • Clausius (macroscopic): dS = δQrev/T for reversible heat exchange.

Both agree in equilibrium. The microscopic version explains why entropy tends to increase: systems evolve toward macrostates with more microstates (more probable).

⭐ Q10.4: What are the modes of heat transfer?

Model answer: (this appeared in interview reference for “Yara”):

  • Conduction: heat transfer through a stationary medium by molecular interactions. Fourier’s law: q =  − κ ∇T.
  • Convection: heat transfer by bulk motion of a fluid.
  • Radiation: heat transfer by electromagnetic waves; Stefan-Boltzmann law P = σAT4.

Chapter 11 — Thermodynamics 2: Laws and Engines

What this chapter covers

  • First law: energy conservation
  • Second law: entropy growth
  • Heat engines, Carnot cycle, efficiency limits

Subject knowledge expected

  • The four laws of thermodynamics
  • Carnot cycle and Carnot efficiency η = 1 − TC/TH
  • Thermodynamic potentials: U, F, H, G

Likely questions

⭐⭐⭐ Q11.1: State the laws of thermodynamics.

Model answer:

  • Zeroth: If A and B are each in thermal equilibrium with C, then A and B are in equilibrium with each other. Defines temperature.
  • First: dU = δQ − δW. Energy conservation including heat.
  • Second: dS ≥ 0 for an isolated system. Entropy of the universe never decreases.
  • Third: S → 0 as T → 0 for a perfect crystal (Nernst).

⭐⭐ Q11.2: What’s the Carnot efficiency?

Model answer: For a heat engine operating between hot reservoir at TH and cold reservoir at TC (in Kelvin): $$\eta_\text{Carnot} = 1 - \dfrac{T_C}{T_H}$$ No engine can exceed this (consequence of the second law). The Carnot cycle (two isothermals + two adiabats) achieves this maximum.

Follow-up Q11.2a: What’s the adiabatic index γ?

Ratio CP/CV. For an ideal monatomic gas: γ = 5/3. For diatomic: γ = 7/5. For radiation (relevant to cosmology — Heisenberg!): γ = 4/3.

⭐⭐ Q11.3: Why does entropy always increase?

Model answer: Statistical argument: macrostates with more microstates are more probable. A system evolving microscopically (deterministically and reversibly!) under Hamilton’s equations explores phase space, and macroscopically it tends toward the most probable macrostate — which is the one with maximum Ω, i.e., maximum S. The increase is statistical, not deterministic — Poincaré recurrence guarantees rare returns, but on cosmological timescales for macroscopic systems.

Chapter 12 — Gauging (Eichung)

What this chapter covers

  • Gauge freedom in electrodynamics
  • Gauge transformations in classical mechanics (less common)
  • Gauge transformations in quantum mechanics
  • Gauge theory as a unifying framework

Subject knowledge expected

  • Aμ → Aμ + ∂μχ leaves observable fields invariant
  • Common gauge choices (Lorenz, Coulomb)
  • Gauge transformation of QM wavefunctions: ψ → eiqχ/ℏψ
  • Local U(1) symmetry ⇒ existence of EM field

Likely questions

⭐⭐⭐ Q12.1: What is a gauge transformation?

Model answer: In EM: Aμ → Aμ + ∂μχ where χ(x) is an arbitrary scalar function. This leaves Fμν = ∂μAν − ∂νAμ unchanged (because partial derivatives commute), so E and B are invariant. The vector potential Aμ contains gauge-dependent (unphysical) information; Fμν is gauge-invariant (physical).

Follow-up Q12.1a: Common gauge choices?

  • Lorenz gauge: ∂μAμ = 0. Lorentz-invariant, useful in covariant treatments.
  • Coulomb gauge: ∇ ⋅ A = 0. Useful for static problems; in this gauge, φ obeys Poisson’s equation directly.
  • Temporal gauge: A0 = 0.

⭐⭐⭐ Q12.2: Why must charge be conserved if EM has gauge invariance?

Model answer: (Already covered in Ch 6.) The interaction Lagrangian  − AμJμ transforms under Aμ → Aμ + ∂μχ to give an extra  − ∂μχJμ. By integration by parts (assuming fields vanish at boundary), this equals  + χ ∂μJμ. For the action to be invariant for arbitrary χ: ∂μJμ = 0 — charge conservation. This is Noether’s theorem applied to local U(1) symmetry.

⭐⭐⭐ Q12.3: How does gauge transform a quantum wavefunction?

Model answer: ψ → eiqχ(x)/ℏ ψ — a local phase rotation depending on position. The kinetic term must use the covariant derivative Dμ = ∂μ + iqAμ/ℏ to be gauge-covariant, instead of plain ∂μ. So the requirement of local U(1) gauge invariance in QM forces the existence of a vector field Aμ with the right transformation — i.e., the photon exists because of U(1) gauge symmetry.

Follow-up Q12.3a: Generalize to non-Abelian gauge theories.

Replace U(1) by a non-Abelian group like SU(2) or SU(3). The gauge field becomes matrix-valued: Aμ = AμaTa with Ta generators. Field strength gets an extra commutator term Fμν = ∂μAν − ∂νAμ − ig[Aμ,Aν]. Gauge fields self-interact (unlike photons). Foundation of the Standard Model: SU(3) × SU(2) × U(1).

Chapter 13 — Quantum Field Theory (★★★ EICHHORN’S DOMAIN)

What this chapter covers

  • Quantization of a free field
  • Interaction picture, S-matrix, perturbation theory
  • Canonical formalism and path integral

Subject knowledge expected

  • Why we need QFT: relativity + QM (variable particle number, locality)
  • Field operators as infinite collections of harmonic oscillators
  • Creation/annihilation operators â† and â
  • Vacuum state, Fock space, particle number operator
  • S-matrix as scattering amplitude generator

Likely questions

⭐⭐⭐ Q13.1: Why do we need quantum field theory?

Model answer: Two reasons (from Eichhorn’s own notes Ch 1):

  1. Relativistic energy fluctuations create particles. From ΔE Δt ≥ ℏ/2 and E = mc2, energy fluctuations on short timescales can produce particle-antiparticle pairs. So a fixed-particle-number theory (single-particle QM) is incomplete.
  2. Causality demands locality. Spacelike-separated measurements must be independent (commute). Standard QM doesn’t naturally have this; QFT builds it in via local field operators.

The fundamental object is a field (Lorentz-invariant), and particles emerge as quantized excitations of the field. This is inspired by electrodynamics: photons are excitations of the EM field.

Follow-up Q13.1a: How does this relate to electrodynamics?

Electrodynamics is already a relativistic field theory. The photoelectric effect shows the field has particle-like excitations (photons). QFT generalizes this to all fields — the electron field gives electrons, the Higgs field gives Higgs bosons, etc.

⭐⭐⭐ Q13.2: How is a free field quantized?

Model answer: For a free scalar field ϕ(x):

  1. Fourier expand into modes: $\phi(\mathbf{x}) = \int \dfrac{d^3p}{(2\pi)^3}\,e^{i\mathbf{p}\cdot\mathbf{x}}\,\tilde\phi(\mathbf{p})$.
  2. Each Fourier mode satisfies a harmonic oscillator equation with frequency $\omega_p = \sqrt{\mathbf{p}^2 + m^2}$.
  3. Promote ϕ and conjugate momentum π to operators with commutation relation [ϕ(x),π(y)] = iℏ δ3(x−y).
  4. Introduce creation/annihilation operators â†(p) and â(p) for each momentum mode.
  5. Fock space: vacuum |0⟩ with â(p)|0⟩ = 0; one-particle states â†(p)|0⟩; multi-particle states by repeated application of â†’s.

So the quantum field is an infinite collection of harmonic oscillators, one per momentum mode. Excitations are particles with definite momentum.

Follow-up Q13.2a: What’s the zero-point energy?

Each mode contributes $\tfrac12 \hbar\omega_p$. Total vacuum energy is divergent (∫d3p ωp). This is the origin of one of the deepest puzzles: the cosmological constant problem — naive QFT predicts vacuum energy  ∼ 10120 times larger than observed dark energy. (Bridges directly to Heisenberg’s territory.)

⭐⭐⭐ Q13.3: What is the path integral formulation?

Model answer: Feynman’s reformulation of QM: ⟨xf, tf|xi, ti⟩ = ∫𝒟x(t) eiS[x]/ℏ The amplitude to go from (xi,ti) to (xf,tf) is a sum over all paths, each weighted by phase eiS/ℏ.

The classical limit (ℏ → 0) recovers Newton: paths near the stationary action δS = 0 interfere constructively; all other paths cancel. This is why the classical action principle is “natural” — it emerges from QM by stationary-phase approximation.

Follow-up Q13.3a: Why path integrals over canonical quantization?

Two big advantages: - Manifestly Lorentz-covariant (no preferred time direction in S). - Symmetries are explicit in S, easier to maintain. - Connection to statistical mechanics: in Euclidean time (t →  − iτ), the path integral becomes a partition function Z = ∫𝒟ϕ e−SE/ℏ. Basis of lattice QFT.

⭐⭐⭐ Q13.4: What is the S-matrix?

Model answer: The S-matrix maps incoming asymptotic states (long before interaction) to outgoing states (long after): |f⟩=Ŝ|i⟩. Its matrix elements ⟨f|Ŝ|i⟩ give scattering amplitudes, which connect to cross sections measurable at particle colliders.

Computed perturbatively in the coupling constant via Feynman diagrams — each diagram represents a term in a series expansion of the S-matrix.

Chapter 14 — General Relativity (★★★ HEISENBERG’S DOMAIN)

What this chapter covers

  • Light cone, equivalence principle, and the metric
  • Connection and curvature
  • Energy-momentum tensor
  • Einstein’s field equations

Subject knowledge expected

  • The equivalence principle: gravitational mass = inertial mass
  • Why this implies gravity is geometry, not a force
  • The metric tensor gμν as the dynamical variable
  • Curvature: Riemann tensor, Ricci tensor, Ricci scalar
  • Einstein’s equations: $R_{\mu\nu} - \tfrac12 g_{\mu\nu} R + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi G T_{\mu\nu}/c^4$
  • The energy-momentum tensor as source of curvature

Likely questions

⭐⭐⭐ Q14.1: State the equivalence principle.

Model answer: Three equivalent forms (increasing strength):

  1. Weak equivalence principle (WEP): All bodies fall with the same acceleration in a gravitational field, regardless of their mass or composition. (Galileo’s observation; Eötvös experiments verified to 10−13.)
  2. Einstein equivalence principle (EEP): Locally (in a small enough region of spacetime), the effects of gravity are indistinguishable from acceleration. A freely falling observer experiences no gravity locally.
  3. Strong equivalence principle (SEP): All laws of physics (including gravity itself!) in a freely falling frame reduce to special relativity locally.

Follow-up Q14.1a: Why does this imply gravity is geometry, not a force?

Because if free fall locally eliminates gravity, then the “natural” trajectories in gravity are geodesics of spacetime — straight lines in the absence of forces. What looks like an attractive force is actually objects following geodesics in curved spacetime. Gravity = curvature. Mass and energy curve spacetime; spacetime tells matter how to move.

Follow-up Q14.1b: Has the equivalence principle been tested?

Yes, to extraordinary precision. Eötvös-type experiments confirm WEP to one part in 1013. The MICROSCOPE satellite mission (2017) pushed this to  ∼ 10−15. All major experiments (lunar laser ranging, atomic interferometry, gravitational redshift) confirm EEP.

Examiner note (Heisenberg): ⭐⭐⭐ Gold-tier topic. If she asks any “why gravity?” question, the equivalence principle is the headline answer.

⭐⭐⭐ Q14.2: What is the metric gμν?

Model answer: The metric tensor encodes spacetime geometry. The infinitesimal interval between two events: ds2 = gμν dxμ dxν In flat spacetime, gμν = ημν = diag(+1,−1,−1,−1) (Minkowski). In curved spacetime, gμν depends on position and is the dynamical field of GR. The metric also raises/lowers indices and defines lengths, angles, and causal structure.

Follow-up Q14.2a: What does it mean for spacetime to be curved?

Geodesics no longer extend to parallel lines that never meet. Two initially parallel geodesics can converge (positive curvature, like on a sphere — gravitational attraction) or diverge (negative curvature). The Riemann tensor R νρσμ measures this geodesic deviation. In flat space, R = 0.

⭐⭐⭐ Q14.3: Write Einstein’s field equations.

Model answer: $$R_{\mu\nu} - \tfrac12 g_{\mu\nu} R + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = \dfrac{8\pi G}{c^4}\,T_{\mu\nu}$$

Left side: geometry (Ricci tensor Rμν, Ricci scalar R, cosmological constant Λ). Right side: matter (energy-momentum tensor Tμν).

“Matter tells spacetime how to curve; spacetime tells matter how to move.” — Wheeler.

Follow-up Q14.3a: What is the energy-momentum tensor for a perfect fluid?

Tμν = (ρ+p/c2) uμuν − p gμν where ρ is rest-frame energy density, p is pressure, uμ is the four-velocity of the fluid. Conservation: ∇μTμν = 0 — covariant continuity equation. Used everywhere in cosmology (e.g., FLRW cosmology models matter and radiation as perfect fluids).

Follow-up Q14.3b: Why is the cosmological constant Λ controversial?

Observed value: Λ ∼ 10−52 m−2 — drives the accelerated expansion of the universe (1998 supernova observations, Nobel 2011). QFT predicts a vacuum energy ~10120 times larger. This cosmological constant problem is one of the deepest puzzles in physics and is a key motivator for modified gravity theories, including Heisenberg’s generalized Proca theories.

⭐⭐⭐ Q14.4: What are gravitational waves?

Model answer: Ripples in spacetime curvature, propagating at the speed of light. They arise as solutions of the linearized Einstein equations gμν = ημν + hμν (small perturbation hμν). In a suitable gauge (transverse-traceless), hμν satisfies a wave equation □hμν = 0. Two polarizations: + and ×.

Follow-up Q14.4a: Why are gravitational waves quadrupole rather than dipole?

Dipole radiation requires a time-varying dipole moment. For mass, the dipole is the position of the center of mass — conserved (momentum conservation). For mass currents, dipole would be angular momentum — also conserved. The lowest non-zero multipole is the quadrupole. (Contrast EM: dipole radiation exists because electric dipole moment isn’t conserved.)

Follow-up Q14.4b: How are they detected?

Laser interferometers like LIGO and Virgo measure strain h ∼ ΔL/L. Typical strains from astrophysical sources: h ∼ 10−21 — fractional length change of one part in 1021 over the 4 km arms. First direct detection: GW150914 (September 14, 2015), a binary black hole merger. Nobel Prize 2017.

Examiner note (Heisenberg): ⭐⭐⭐ GW physics is directly in her field — modified gravity theories produce different polarization modes (up to 6 instead of 2). If she asks about GW polarization, mention you’re aware that modified gravity can predict more than two polarizations — that’s gold for her.

⭐⭐⭐ Q14.5: What is a black hole?

Model answer: A region of spacetime where gravity is so strong that not even light can escape. The boundary is the event horizon, at Schwarzschild radius rs = 2GM/c2. For a 1 M⊙ black hole, rs ≈ 3 km.

Follow-up Q14.5a: What’s the no-hair theorem?

Stationary black hole solutions are characterized by only three parameters: mass, charge, angular momentum (and nothing else). All other information about matter that formed the black hole is lost behind the horizon. This is a major motivation for studying black hole solutions in modified gravity (do they still obey no-hair?).

Follow-up Q14.5b: What’s Hawking radiation?

Black holes radiate thermally with temperature TH = ℏc3/(8πGMkB). Smaller black holes are hotter. Information paradox: thermal radiation seems to carry no information about what fell in — open problem at the intersection of GR and QFT.

⭐⭐ Q15.1: How has our conception of space and time evolved?

Strategic tips, distilled

  1. Lead with keywords. “We use separation of variables and a typical ansatz” is better than a five-minute setup.
  2. Use natural units if appropriate. If you say ℏ = 1 explicitly, they’ll smile.
  3. Symmetries are your best friend. Half the questions can be answered “because of the symmetry” plus one elaborating sentence.
  4. Bridge classical and quantum when possible. They love “Poisson brackets become commutators.” “Classical action is the saddle point of the quantum path integral.”
  5. Bridge SR and GR when possible. They love “Special relativity is the case of flat spacetime; in general spacetime, free fall = geodesics in curved spacetime.”
  6. Acknowledge the cosmological connection. Mentioning the cosmological constant problem, dark energy, gravitational waves, CMB scores points with Heisenberg.
  7. Acknowledge the quantization connection. Mentioning canonical quantization, path integrals, Fock space, gauge invariance scores points with Eichhorn.
  8. Be honest about limits. “I haven’t worked through that calculation, but the structure is…” beats bluffing every time.

What NOT to do

  • Don’t drone on with a long derivation when a keyword suffices.
  • Don’t pretend to know advanced topics (renormalization, Hawking radiation details, etc.) in technical detail. Recognize them and gesture at the idea.
  • Don’t ignore ℏ or factors silently — note your units choice openly.
  • Don’t argue about edge cases that don’t help your answer.
